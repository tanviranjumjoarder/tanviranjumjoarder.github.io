01 / Biography

About the researcher

Tanvir Anjum Joarder is an incoming doctoral researcher in the DeGiorgio Lab at the Department of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science, Florida Atlantic University. He joins the program in Fall 2026 to pursue a PhD in Computer Science with a research focus on statistical and machine learning methods for population genomic inference.

He received his Bachelor of Science in Computer Science and Engineering from Rajshahi University of Engineering and Technology (RUET), Bangladesh, in 2019. His undergraduate thesis, supervised by Professor A. H. M. Sarowar Sattar, investigated supervised classification techniques for the early detection of hepatocellular carcinoma from longitudinal online search query patterns. The work was published at the 4th IEEE International Conference on Electrical, Information and Communication Technology in 2019.

Prior to commencing doctoral study, he served as Assistant General Manager in the Department of Information Technology at the Bangladesh Rural Electrification Board, where he managed national scale consumer billing analytics, geographic information systems for distribution infrastructure, transformer load management, and complaint registration systems serving the rural electricity consumers of Bangladesh.

His current research interests include convolutional and tensor based deep learning architectures for selective sweep detection, transfer learning across demographic histories, statistical inference of natural selection, and the application of contemporary machine learning methods to genomic populations historically underrepresented in published genomic studies. He has a particular interest in the analysis of South Asian genomic diversity.

02 / Research Interests

Areas of inquiry

Methodological research at the interface of machine learning, statistical inference, and computational evolutionary genomics.

R1

Deep Learning for Selection Detection

Convolutional and tensor based neural architectures trained on coalescent simulations to classify selective sweeps (hard, soft, balancing, and adaptive introgression) from raw haplotype data treated as multi channel image representations.

R2

Transfer Learning Across Demographic Histories

Domain adaptation methods that close the simulation to reality gap for population genomic inference, enabling models trained on one demographic backdrop to generalise to novel populations and divergent evolutionary histories.

R3

South Asian Population Genomics

Methodological extensions of selection inference and demographic modelling to South Asian populations, which constitute approximately one quarter of the world's population yet remain substantially underrepresented in published genome data.

R4

Statistical Genetics & Coalescent Theory

Likelihood based and summary statistic scans for natural selection (SweepFinder, BALLET, VolcanoFinder family), and the underlying coalescent and site frequency spectrum theory that supplies the null comparisons for machine learning methods.

R5

Reproducible Bioinformatics

Workflow engineering with Snakemake and Nextflow, containerised software, and HPC scheduling with Slurm. Emphasis on producing reusable, well documented, and reproducible methodological implementations.

R6

Machine Learning for Biomedical Signal

Earlier work on supervised classification of hepatocellular carcinoma from longitudinal search query data, demonstrating the applicability of standard machine learning methods to noisy and temporally structured biomedical signals.

05 / Selected Projects

Engineering and research projects

Research · 2019

Liver Cancer Detection from Online Search Logs

Designed an end to end supervised learning pipeline that curated symptom query datasets temporally aligned with disease progression stages, engineered features capturing temporal query patterns, and benchmarked six classifiers: Support Vector Machine (94.30 percent), Random Forest (97.50 percent), Decision Tree (95.80 percent), Neural Network (93.50 percent), Naïve Bayes (90.70 percent), and k Nearest Neighbour (85.50 percent). Published at IEEE EICT 2019.
